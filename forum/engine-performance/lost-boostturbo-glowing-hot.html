Hi again reves,

what type of hit did you have with the snow bank (guessing side vehicle impact) as it would help knowing how the car hit so the direction of impact might point us in  a better direction for diagnosis?

Since you have a stock intercooler system it's alot less likely to be a hose leak which are common with front mount intercoolers.

If your turbo was glowing it could just be from beating on her at the track. The loss of boost is more of a concern as I've seen many a glowing turbo before (almost to the point where it looks almost translucent and it seems like you can almost see into it)

If your turbo is glowing and you have a loss of boost it could be a couple of things like a bad turbo which is now making impeller contact with the housing (this would cause an  audible sound you'd probably hear if this happened as it sounds really weird). You can unbolt the intake tube from the front of the turbo to reach in and feel the play of the impeller wheel and see how it rotates. The rotation should be perfectly smooth (if not the blades are touching the housing and you can throw that turbo away). Now you can also check for clearance on the shaft both in and out from the intake and exhaust side to side plus vertical/horizontal movement which could cause the impeller blades to touch.

Now the other only applies if you have a catalytic converter stil on the vehicle. What could happen is the catalytic converter can either melt slightly or even shift inside the exhaust which would hinder airflow through the exhaust. The added backpressure between the catalytic and the engine slows down the flow rate and of course gives you a low boost scenario which of course since the heated gasses can't escape in a timely manner the heat from the exhaust builds in this pressurized zone and gives you a possible glowing exhaust. If it is the catalytic converter replace it with a test pipe and you'll get even faster spool response from the turbo... just bad emissions from the exhaust. This of course could also be the same result of a pinch exhaust along the pipe which could essentially do the same thing.

The only other thing which would give you this condition of a glowing exhaust is a lean air fuel mixture (but this would not give you a low boost scenario) so I don't think this would apply and even if you hit the snow bank and there was a slosh moment where the fuel pressure died it's not long enough to create a "glowing" situation with the exhaust.

Hope this helps

Give us as much info as possible and of course this will help in determining what the issue could be.
